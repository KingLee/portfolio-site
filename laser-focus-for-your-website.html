Laser Focus For Your Website.

Taking a moment to consider your content.

"Everything in Life has a Purpose" - Some Wise Guy

A website is no exception.

When deciding that you need a website, or that you need to update your website there should be a single reason for taking the steps necessary to bring a fresh look to your online presence… That's your conversion. For some, a conversion may be business leads, or for an ecommerce site it would be the sale of an item. Every site has a goal, and goals may vary depending on what kind of business you run. Ask yourself, what function does your website serve in achieving your goal?

One thing remains the same throughout all websites: Don't get in the way of the conversion.

The more elements you add to a website, the more attention you call away from the goal. The object of conversion-based design is focus. What does this mean? It means boiling down your content to essential information and then presenting that information in a way that leads the user to meaningfully interact with that information. The less essential information you have, the easier it will be for users to understand and interact with, leading to more conversions.

Taking a hard look at what you think is important, and what is actually important may be tough for some people. A lot of people have an emotional attachment to content on their website or marketing materials, providing a large distraction from the goal at hand. Don't be afraid to cut down your content and hone your message! More than likely, your site or marketing materials need a good trimming.

"Focusing is not about saying yes, it is about saying no" - Sir Steve Jobs

Giving the user fewer options, hones focus. Think about it. You have the power to direct them wherever you want, based on your content. Direct them towards a path that directly benefits your business; not other companies, or their own reading comprehension skills (That's a content length joke.)

Options to focus your content could take the form of:

1. Fewer Navigation Items (i.e. eliminate useless pages and content… consolidate and combine similar content).

2. Limit or remove non-essential User Paths whenever possible. (i.e. related links, breadcrumbs, footer navigations, non-goal related sidebar callouts).

3. Limiting lists to relevant points. (This is the ironic part of the post…)

There are a bunch of small design-y things you can do to focus content as well, but the main message here is… the message. A lot of companies won't hone the message well enough, and lose or confuse people within the first interaction of their brand.

Don't be those people.
